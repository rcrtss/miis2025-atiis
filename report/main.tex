\begin{abstract}
This work tries to integrate a formal framework for blameworthiness apportionment in multi-agent settings with the statistical reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs). We replicate the Friedenberg-Halpern (FH) framework for blameworthiness in a committee of voters scenario, performing a parameter sensitivity analysis to understand how blameworthiness varies with belief, vote, switch cost, stakes, pressure effectiveness, threshold, and group size. We then evaluate LLM-based judges in the same scenario, assessing whether their blame assignments align with the FH framework's predictions. Our findings indicate that while LLMs capture the qualitative structure of blameworthiness, they exhibit quantitative miscalibration, highlighting both the potential and limitations of using LLMs for responsibility assessment in multi-agent contexts.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Perspectives on Blame and Responsibility}

% - Open by saying blameworthiness is not a single notion; different traditions emphasize control, alternatives, intention, causality, knowledge, and group structure.

Throughout the history of philosophy and psychology, the concepts of blame and moral responsibility have been widely studied from different angles. There is no universally accepted notion of blame, and the understanding on how humans apportion blame may vary depending on the context of the situation, traditions, and the subject's perspective. Despite this ambiguity, the concept of blame is commonly related to terms like intention, intervention, knowledge, control, alternatives, and group structure.

% - Use Frankfurt to mark the classic shift away from “could have done otherwise” as the core condition for responsibility.

Historically, the \textit{Principle of Alternate Possibilities} had widely accepted to determine if a person is moral responsible for an action on a given context. Roughly put, this principle states that an agent is morally responsible for an action only if they could have done otherwise. This perspective effectively focuses on the \textit{circumstances}, that is, if the circumstances (or external options) dictate that it is impossible for such an agent to have done otherwise, then automatically that agent is excused from moral responsibility.

Opposing to this view, Harry G. Frankfurt proposed in 1969 a revision on this principle that shifted the focus from \textit{circumstances} towards \textit{motivations} \cite{frankfurt_alternate_2003}. His revised principle looks at the internal reasoning of the agent, instead of only looking at the context of the situation. He argued that an agent is not morally responsible for what he has done if he did it \textit{only} because he could not have done otherwise.

% Parragraph below: keep?
This new perspective lies at the core of what is known in philosophy as \textit{compatibilism}, a widely accepted philosophical current that has reframed the notion of moral responsibility in recent decades. Contrary to \textit{incompatibilism}, which claims that free will and causal determinism are mutually exclusive, \textit{compatibilism} consists on the belief that free will and determinism as mutually compatible and argues that, even if determinism is true, we still have a meaningful type of free will and moral responsibility.

% - Use Fischer and Ravizza to show the control-based view: responsibility depends on actual guidance or ownership of the action.
% --> The view of moral responsibility presented develops a distinction between two different types of control – regulative control and guidance control – discussed in Fischer’s earlier book The Metaphysics of Free Will.2 An agent has regulative control over her action when she has alternative actions available to her. Agents can have guidance control even when they have no alternative possibilities available to them. Fischer and Ravizza claim that guidance control is what is required for moral responsibility,3 and argue, also, that guidance control is compatible with causal determinism. This means, as Fischer and Ravizza stress, that moral responsibility is derived from what actually happens in the causal sequence leading to an agent’s action, not what could have happened, but didn’t.

Fischer and Ravizza (1998) \cite{fischer_responsibility_1998} presented a different view on moral responsibility by distinguishing between two types of control (in the sense of the ability to decide over one's actions and omissions): regulative control and guidance control. Roughly, an agent has regulative control over their actions when alternative actions are available for the agent to take, and they have guidance control even when the agent has no alternative possibilities available. The authors claim that moral responsibility depends on the latter, and that guidance control is also compatible with causal determinism. In other words, they argue that moral responsibility derives from what actually happens in the causal sequence leading to an agent's actions, not what could have happened, but didn't.

% - Add Malle as the psychological angle: blame is shaped by perceived intent, causality, foreseeability, and preventability.
This philosophical idea that an agent can still be blamed for a bad action even if they had absolutely no other choice, was tested on a psychological level by Malle et al. (2014) \cite{malle_theory_2014}. Malle's ``Path Model'' proved that, when humans judge someone, our brains intuitively act like detectives moving through a mental checklist: we figure out if they caused the harm, if they meant to do it, and what their internal reasons were.

% - Move to Chockler and Halpern for the formal causal account: blame and responsibility can be measured in degrees using structural models.
To formalize the notion of responsibility and blame, Hannah Chockler and Joe Halpern (2004) \cite{chockler_responsibility_2004} came up with an elegant approach based on structural-models, shifting the perspective about causality from a binary concept into a graded degree of responsibility. The main idea of such framework lies on calculating the minimum number of counterfactual changes required for an agent's action to become the critical cause of an outcome. They additionally separate the terms \textit{responsibility} and \textit{blame} by incorporating an epistemic state. In other words, the authors propose a perspective where the blame of an agent is evaluated on what the agent actually knew (or believed), or should have known, before acting.

% - Then Halpern and Kleiman-Weiner: blame also depends on the agent’s epistemic state, not just causation.
Furthermore, Halpern and Kleiman-Weiner (2018) \cite{halpern_towards_2018} expand this idea by integrating the ideas of an expected \text{impact} of one's actions on an outcome, as well as the \textit{cost} of taking alternate actions. Specifically, they argue that agent's blameworthiness should be mitigated if preventing a negative outcome would have required an unreasonably high personal cost (e.g., risking one's life). Additionally, they define intention as the result of testing weather an agent would have chosen a different action if they believed their actions had no impact on the outcome.

% - End with Friedenberg and Halpern: in multiagent settings, blame must be assigned to groups and then distributed to individuals, since outcomes are jointly produced.
Finally, Friedenberg and Halpern (2019) \cite{friedenberg_blameworthiness_2019} extend this framework from individual blameworthiness assessment to complex, multi-agent scenarios, such as the ``Tragedy of the Commons'', where shared outcomes make assigning culpability difficult. Their approach splits blame in two: group blame and individual blame. They determine a group's overall blameworthiness by evaluating the feasibility and cost of the group coordinating to act differently. To split this collective blame among individual actors, they apply the Shapley value from cooperative game theory \cite{shapley_value_nodate}, apportioning blame to each agent based on their marginal contributions to the outcome.

This work builds directly upon the framework proposed by Friedenberg and Halpern (FH from now on), adopting their definitions, methods, and examples. We apply this perspective through an experimental lens to study blameworthiness in complex scenarios, assessing how modern artificial intelligence systems behave compared to this theoretical model.

\subsection{Causal Models and Blameworthiness}

In this and the following section, we present a review of the relevant foundational mathematical ideas and concepts presented in the FH framework regarding blameworthiness in multi-agent settings \cite{friedenberg_blameworthiness_2019}. Throughout this work, we adopt the explanations and notation used by the authors in their paper.

% - Introduce causal models as the formal language used to represent how outcomes depend on agents’ actions.

FH reuse the causal model framework of Halpern and Pearl (2005) \cite{halpern_causes_2005} to build on top of it. Here we outline a review of the topic.

\subsubsection{Actual Causality}

Halpern and Pearl introduce the concept of \textit{actual causality}, and distinguish it from general or type causality. Without going deeper into this distinction, what is relevant is that actual causality refers to the specific relationship between events where one event is considered to directly cause another. It focuses on how particular events influence outcomes in specific contexts, and allows for a nuanced understanding of causation.

\subsubsection{Structural Equations Modelling}

% - Explain that structural models allow one to isolate causal dependence and compare alternative action profiles.
\textit{Structural equations modeling} is a powerful tool used in statistics for modelling how variables affect one another in a system by defining a set of equations. HP (and therefore FH) use this at the core of their causal framework. In this subsection we limit ourselves to a brief overview of the topic, and refer the reader to the original work of Halpern and Pearl (2005) \cite{halpern_causes_2005} for a more detailed explanation.

A \textit{structural equations model} (SEM) has two different types of variables: \textit{exogenous} variables and \textit{endogenous} variables. Exogenous variables are those that, for a specific model, their causes are excluded from the model. Endogenous variables are those variables for which their causes are included in the model (their values are determined by factors in the model), and which can be either endogenous or exogenous themselves.

A classical example used to understand the general idea behind causality, and its representation in SEMs, is as follows. Imagine that Billy and Suzy are playing at throwing rocks to a glass bottle so that it breaks. Each of them have their own rock, and it suffices that one of them hits the bottle with the rock for it to break. Additionally, we assume both throwers have perfect accuracy (if they throw, they hit the bottle). In this case, we could start modelling the system by defining a binary variable \texttt{bottle\_broken} that is initially set to \texttt{False}. Then, we can define two more variables: \texttt{billy\_throws} and \texttt{suzy\_throws}. Intuitively, we can see that \texttt{billy\_throws} and \texttt{suzy\_throws} are causes and that \texttt{bottle\_broken} is the effect. See Figure~\ref{fig:sem_example} to see the SEM diagram.

In this example, if we think of it as a \textit{structural equations model}, the variable \texttt{bottle\_broken} would be an \textit{endogenous} variable, while both \texttt{billy\_throws} and \texttt{suzy\_throws} would be \textit{exogenous} variables. The explanation for this is that we ignore what are the causes of Billy and Suzy throwing the rock (they may have bet on that, they may be bored, they may have stumbled with a bottle and some rocks, etc.), although we know they exist, we simply leave them out of the model. Instead, we do know which are the variables (inside the model) that affect the values of the variable \texttt{bottle\_broken}.

\subsubsection{Mathematical Definition of a Causal Model}

Formally, FH define a causal model $\mathcal{M}$ as a tuple $(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{F})$ where:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{S} = (\mathcal{U},\mathcal{V},\mathcal{R})$: a signature, composed of a finite but nonempty set $\mathcal{U}$ of exogenous variables, a finite but nonempty set $\mathcal{V}$ of endogenous variables, and a range function $\mathcal{R}$ that maps each variable $\mathcal{U} \cup \mathcal{V}$ to a set of possible values.
  \item $\mathcal{F}$: a set of functions that map each endogenous variable $X \in \mathcal{V}$ to a function $F_X$ that determines the value of $X$ based on the values of other variables in $\mathcal{U} \cup \mathcal{V}$. Each function $F_X$ takes as input the values of all other variables in $\mathcal{U} \cup \mathcal{V}$ and outputs a value in $\mathcal{R}(X)$.
  \item Then, $\mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{F})$ is a causal model that represents the relationships between variables in a system, where the functions in $\mathcal{F}$ capture the causal dependencies among the variables.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Causal Models in Multi-Agent Settings}

For scenarios with multiple agents, FH extend the signature of the causal model to be $\mathcal{S} = (\mathcal{U},\mathcal{V},\mathcal{R},\mathcal{G})$, where:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{G}: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow G$ associates an agent $ag \in G$ with an action variable in $\mathcal{A}$, where $G$ is the set of all agents in the system, and $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{V}$ is the set of all (endogenous) action variables in the model. This allows for a clear representation of which agents are responsible for which actions.
  \item The range of a variable $A \in \mathcal{A}$ is the set of actions available to the agent $\mathcal{G}(A)$.
\end{itemize}

For instance, if there are five members in a committee, and each of them can vote \texttt{yes} or \texttt{no}, then there will be action variables $A_1$ through $A_5$ with $\mathcal{G}(A_i) = ag_i$ for $i=1,\ldots,5$, and $\mathcal{R}(A_i) = \{\texttt{yes},\texttt{no}\}$.

Furthermore, FH define a \textit{causal setting} as a tuple $(\mathcal{M},\vec{u})$ where $\mathcal{M}$ is a causal model and $\vec{u}$ is a context that assigns values to the exogenous variables in $\mathcal{U}$. This allows for the evaluation of causal relationships in specific contexts, enabling the analysis of how different agents' actions contribute to outcomes.

\subsubsection{Reasoning About Interventions}

Causal models allow for reasoning about interventions, which are hypothetical scenarios where we can formulate statements like ``if action $a$ was taken, the outcome would have been $\varphi$''. Formally:

\begin{itemize}
  \item An intervention is represented as a pair $(\vec{X} \gets \vec{x})$, where $\vec{X}$ is a set of variables and $\vec{x}$ is a set of values. This represents the action of setting the variables in $\vec{X}$ to the values in $\vec{x}$, regardless of their original values.
  \item The outcome of an intervention is defined with the Greek letter $\varphi$, and represents a Boolean combination of primitive events (e.g., $X=x$) that can be evaluated in the modified causal model after the intervention.
  \item The formula $[A \gets a]\varphi$ can then be read as ``if the action $A$ were set to $a$, then the outcome $\varphi$ would have occurred''.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Blameworthiness}

% - Single agent settings
To answer the question of how blameworthy is an agent $ag$ for an outcome $\varphi$, FH define the notion of \textit{blameworthiness} as a function of the agent's actions, the context, and the causal model. Roughly, the blameworthiness of an agent $ag$ for an outcome $\varphi$ is denoted as $db^c_N(ag,a,\mathcal{E},\varphi)$ and is calculated based on the agent's actions ($a,a'$), the context ($c,N$), and the causal models embedded in the epistemic state of the agent ($\mathcal{E}$).

Namely, we have that:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $ag$ is the agent whose blameworthiness is being evaluated.
  \item $a$ is the action taken by the agent.
  \item $a'$ is the action that the agent could have taken instead.
  \item $c(a)$ maps actions to the cost of taking that action. FH admit that this cost function is not always easy to define, and that it may be subjective and context-dependent. For instance, in a voting scenario, the cost of voting \texttt{yes} or \texttt{no} may depend on the agent's personal beliefs, the social pressure they face, or the potential consequences of their vote.
  \item $\mathcal{E}$ is the epistemic state of the agent, which captures their beliefs about the causal model and the context. Formally, $\mathcal{E} = (\Pr, \mathcal{K})$ is a probability distribution over a set of all causal settings that $ag$ considers possible ($\mathcal{K}$).
  \item $\varphi$ is the outcome for which blameworthiness is being assessed.
  \item $N$ is a balance parameter that trades off between the harm averted, and the cost incurred by the agent's actions. Similar to the cost function $c$, FH mention the difficulty of defining this parameter, and that sometimes it may not be possible to capture it clearly.
\end{itemize}

We then can define how likely an outcome $\varphi$ is to occur if the agent had taken action $a$ instead of $a'$, given an epistemic state $\mathcal{E}$, as $\delta^\mathcal{E}_{a,a',\varphi}$. Although FH do not provide a name for this term, we will refer to it as the \textit{impact} of the agent's action on the outcome $\varphi$ (this name may not be fully descriptive but serves our purposes). The impact of an agent's action on an outcome is therefore defined as:

\begin{equation}
  \delta^\mathcal{E}_{a,a',\varphi} = 
  \max(0, \Pr(\llbracket [ A = a ]\,\varphi \rrbracket_\mathcal{K}) - \Pr(\llbracket [ A = a' ]\,\varphi \rrbracket_\mathcal{K}))
\label{eq:impact}
\end{equation}

where:

\begin{itemize}
  \item For a given causal formula $\psi$, let $\llbracket \psi \rrbracket_\mathcal{K}$ denote the set of all settings in $\mathcal{K}$ where $\psi$ is true. 
  \item Then, $\Pr(\llbracket [ A = a ]\,\varphi \rrbracket_\mathcal{K})$ is the probability that agent $ag$ ascribes to the outcome $\varphi$ occurring given that action $a$ is taken.
\end{itemize}

With this background, the blameworthiness (or degree of blameworthiness) of an agent $ag$ for an outcome $\varphi$, relative to an action $a'$, given that the agent took action $a$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  db^c_N(ag, a, a', \mathcal{E}, \varphi) = \delta^\mathcal{E}_{a,a',\varphi} \cdot \frac{N - \max (\text{c}(a') - \text{c}(a), 0)}{N}
\label{eq:blameworthiness_single_agent}
\end{equation}

Finally, given a taken action $a$, the degree of blameworthiness of an agent $ag$ for an outcome $\varphi$ is defined as the maximum degree of blameworthiness over all possible alternative actions $a'$ that the agent could have taken: 

\begin{equation}
  db^c_N(ag, a, \mathcal{E}, \varphi) = \max_{a'} db^c_N(ag, a, a', \mathcal{E}, \varphi)
\label{eq:blameworthiness_max}
\end{equation}

The intuition behind this definition is as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The first term, $\delta^\mathcal{E}_{a,a',\varphi}$, captures the impact of the agent's action on the outcome $\varphi$. If the agent's action significantly increases the likelihood of the outcome occurring, this term will be larger, indicating a higher degree of blameworthiness.
  \item The second term, $\frac{N - \max (\text{c}(a') - \text{c}(a), 0)}{N}$, captures the cost of taking an alternative action $a'$. If the alternative action is significantly more costly than the taken action, this term will be smaller, reducing the overall degree of blameworthiness.
  \item By taking the maximum over all possible alternative actions $a'$, we ensure that we are considering the most blameworthy scenario for the agent.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{FH Blameworthiness in Multi-Agent Settings}

The previous section presented the notion of blameworthiness in single-agent settings. However, in many real-world scenarios, outcomes are the result of the combined actions of multiple agents. In such cases, it is not sufficient to evaluate blameworthiness solely based on individual actions. Instead, we must consider how groups of agents collectively contribute to an outcome.

FH refer to the ``Tragedy of the Commons'' \cite{hardin_tragedy_1968} as a classic example of a multi-agent setting where blameworthiness must be assessed at the group level. In this scenario, multiple agents exploit a shared resource, leading to its depletion and negative consequences for all. The blameworthiness of each agent cannot be evaluated in isolation, as the outcome is the result of their collective actions.

As useful example, they present the Fishermen problem, where in a lake full of fish and fishermen, it is of common interest that fish don't run out. For one reason or another, all fishermen know that if they fish more than 10 fish on a given year, the fish will effectively run out and there will be no fishes to fish the next year. Still, since all of them also believe that there is a high chance the rest of the fishermen will overfish, then each of them decides to overfish, leading to the common resource running out for the next year. Although it was because the actions of each of the fishermen that the fish ran out, it is reasonable to think that they acted sensibly, since the belief about others overfishing was accurate (in the scenario, it is something that actually happened). 

Therefore, we may think that they cannot be blamed individually. Still, the group as a whole is certainly blameworthy for the outcome, and thus each of them must have a certain degree of blame. This conclusion captures the main idea that motivates an extension to the blameworthiness framework, such as that of FH, where complex multi-agent settings are addressed more appropriately.

FH claim that in order to assign blame to the group (and later on to each individual agent), it is necessary to assess the cost to the fishermen of coordinating their actions (e.g., creating regulations, installing sensors to monitor the number of fish, etc.). FH also mention that computing such costs can be quite difficult in general. To simplify the problem, they directly associate a cost with the set of epistemic states (distributions over causal settings) that are viewed as the possible outcomes of attempts to coordinate. 

By doing so, the framework abstracts away the complex details of real-world collaboration and focuses directly on the expected cost of achieving a more favorable epistemic state. Once this cost and the resulting impact on the outcome are established, it is possible to compute a formal degree of \textit{group blameworthiness}. However, because actions are ultimately taken by individuals, this group-level blame must then be fairly apportioned among the specific agents involved. To achieve this, FH utilize the Shapely \cite{shapley_value_nodate} concept from cooperative game theory to calculate each agent's individual share of the blame based on their marginal contributions to various coalitions. 

To illustrate how these abstract concepts of coordination, costs, and apportionment play out in a more concise scenario, the following sections will formally define these metrics and apply them to a simplified model: a committee of voters \cite{friedenberg_blameworthiness_2019}. In this voting setting, which is the core foundation that this project builds on top of, the complex idea of ``coordinating an alternative epistemic state'' is cleanly reduced to specific, quantifiable actions. In particular, subsets of agents can form coalitions to apply social pressure (for a specific cost) to increase the probability of a positive vote from others, or an individual can incur a personal cost to definitively switch their own vote. This abstraction provides an ideal test environment to explore the mathematical nuances of the FH framework without getting lost in the intractable variables of real-world coordination. More about this is detailed in the next sections.

\subsubsection{Group Blameworthiness}

To extend the blameworthiness framework to settings where multiple agents can coordinate, FH shift the focus from individual actions to \textit{epistemic states} which pertain to collective actions. The degree of blameworthiness for a group of agents $Ag'$ for an outcome $\varphi$ is defined as $gb^c_N(Ag',\mathcal{E}_1,\varphi)$ and is calculated based on the actual epistemic state ($\mathcal{E}_1$), potential alternative epistemic states ($\mathcal{E}_2$), and the cost of coordination.

Specifically, we have that:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $Ag'$ is the subset of agents whose collective blameworthiness is being evaluated, where $Ag' \subseteq Ag$ and $Ag$ is the set of all agents.
  \item $\mathcal{E}_1 = (\Pr_1, \mathcal{K}_1)$ is the actual epistemic state of the group.
  \item $\mathcal{E}_2 = (\Pr_2, \mathcal{K}_2)$ is the alternative epistemic state that the group could have brought about through coordination.
  \item $c(Ag', \mathcal{E})$ maps a subset of agents and an epistemic state to the expected cost of the coordination actions required for the agents in $Ag'$ to bring about $\mathcal{E}$.
  \item $\varphi$ is the outcome for which blameworthiness is being assessed.
  \item $N$ is the balance parameter trading off between the harm averted and the cost incurred.
\end{itemize}

Similar to the single-agent setting, we define the \textit{impact} of moving from epistemic state $\mathcal{E}_1$ to $\mathcal{E}_2$ on the outcome $\varphi$. This represents how much more likely $\varphi$ was under the actual state compared to the alternative state:

\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\mathcal{E}_1,\mathcal{E}_2,\varphi} = \max(0, \text{Pr}_1(\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\mathcal{K}_1}) - \text{Pr}_2(\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_{\mathcal{K}_2}))
\label{eq:impact_group}
\end{equation}

With this background, the degree of blameworthiness of a group $Ag'$ for an outcome $\varphi$, relative to an alternative epistemic state $\mathcal{E}_2$, given the actual epistemic state $\mathcal{E}_1$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  gb^c_N(Ag', \mathcal{E}_1, \mathcal{E}_2, \varphi) = \delta_{\mathcal{E}_1,\mathcal{E}_2,\varphi} \cdot \frac{N - \max (c(Ag', \mathcal{E}_2) - c(Ag', \mathcal{E}_1), 0)}{N}
\label{eq:blameworthiness_group_relative}
\end{equation}

Finally, given an actual epistemic state $\mathcal{E}_1$, the degree of blameworthiness of a group $Ag'$ for an outcome $\varphi$ is defined as the maximum degree of blameworthiness over all possible alternative epistemic states $\mathcal{E}_2$ that the group could have coordinated to achieve (where the cost $c(Ag', \mathcal{E}_2)$ is finite):

\begin{equation}
  gb^c_N(Ag', \mathcal{E}_1, \varphi) = \max_{\mathcal{E}_2} gb^c_N(Ag', \mathcal{E}_1, \mathcal{E}_2, \varphi)
\label{eq:blameworthiness_group_max}
\end{equation}

The intuition behind this definition strongly parallels the single-agent case:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The first term, $\delta_{\mathcal{E}_1,\mathcal{E}_2,\varphi}$, captures the impact of the group's coordination on the outcome $\varphi$. If an alternative coordination strategy significantly decreases the likelihood of the negative outcome, this term grows, indicating higher blame for failing to coordinate.
  \item The second term captures the cost penalty. If the alternative coordination state $\mathcal{E}_2$ is significantly more costly to achieve than the actual state $\mathcal{E}_1$, the overall degree of group blameworthiness is reduced.
  \item By taking the maximum over all finite-cost alternative states $\mathcal{E}_2$, we identify the most blameworthy failure of coordination for that specific group.
\end{itemize}

An important note from FH is regarding \textit{whose} epistemic state is being considered. So far we have not defined which agent's beliefs are used to evaluate the probabilities in the impact term. For individual blameworthiness, it is clear that the agent's own epistemic state should be used. However, for group blameworthiness, FH argue that it is reasonable to use the epistemic state of the \textit{society}, which they define as the view of what a reasonable agent would believe about the situation. The ambiguity of this definition is acknowledged, but FH suggest that it is a reasonable approximation for evaluating collective blameworthiness in multi-agent settings, and it is one we can work with for this project's purposes.

\subsubsection{Apportioning Blame Among Agents}

Once the blameworthiness of a group is established, the next natural question is how this collective blame should be divided among its individual members. To solve this, FH utilize a standard concept from cooperative game theory: the Shapley value.

To apply the Shapley value, we first must define the \textit{marginal contribution} of an individual agent $ag_j$ to a specific coalition of agents $Ag'$. This measures how much the group's blameworthiness increases when agent $ag_j$ is included in the coalition. For an agent $ag_j \in Ag'$, their marginal contribution is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  mb^{c,\mathcal{E}}_N(j, Ag', \varphi) = gb^c_N(Ag', \mathcal{E}, \varphi) - gb^c_N(Ag' \setminus \{ag_j\}, \mathcal{E}, \varphi)
\label{eq:marginal_contribution}
\end{equation}

Similarly, if $ag_j \notin Ag'$, their marginal contribution is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  mb^{c,\mathcal{E}}_N(j, Ag', \varphi) = gb^c_N(Ag' \cup \{ag_j\}, \mathcal{E}, \varphi) - gb^c_N(Ag', \mathcal{E}, \varphi)
\label{eq:marginal_contribution_not_in_coalition}
\end{equation}

Using these marginal contributions, the individual degree of blameworthiness for an agent $ag_j$ (denoted as $db^{c,\mathcal{E}}_N(j, \varphi)$) is calculated by taking a weighted sum of their marginal contributions to all possible coalitions they could be a part of:

\begin{equation}
  db^{c,\mathcal{E}}_N(j, \varphi) = \sum_{\{Ag' \subseteq Ag : ag_j \in Ag'\}} \frac{(|Ag'|-1)! (|Ag|-|Ag'|)!}{|Ag|!} mb^{c,\mathcal{E}}_N(j, Ag', \varphi)
\label{eq:shapley_blame}
\end{equation}

The intuition behind this apportionment is as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The Shapley value essentially calculates the average marginal contribution of the agent across all possible sub-coalitions they could join.
  \item The fractional term acts as a combinatorial weight, satisfying the \textit{Efficiency} axiom \cite{friedenberg_blameworthiness_2019}, which ensures that the total blame distributed among individuals equals the total group blameworthiness of the entire set of agents $Ag$.
  \item If an agent's participation in a coalition drastically changes the group's ability to prevent the outcome (e.g., they have a low personal cost to change their vote), their marginal contribution and thus their individual share of the blame will be proportionally higher.
\end{itemize}

As AI systems (e.g., large language models) are increasingly adopted by society, it becomes crucial to address questions about accountability and responsibility. Dastani et al. (2023)~\cite{dastani_responsibility_2023} argue that as autonomous AI systems become integrated into society, it is necessary to embed formal responsibility models directly into their reasoning processes. When it comes to recent powerful generative models, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), questions arise about whether these systems should be adopted in critical decision-making contexts, and if so, how to ensure that they are used responsibly. The stochastic nature of LLMs, combined with their ability to generate outputs that can influence human decisions, raises concerns about the potential for harm and the difficulty of assigning responsibility when things go wrong.

\subsubsection{Large Language Models as Agents}

A particular concern being actively discussed involves the accountability when it comes to problems involving collective decisions and actions. This problem is commonly known as the ``many hands problem'', which occurs when it becomes difficult to identify individual's causal contribution to an outcome within a collective decision-process~\cite{dastani_responsibility_2023}. Whether we talk about human-AI teams, agentic AI systems (AI-driven systems that can take actions and influence outcomes), or multi-agent systems (where AI systems may act autonomously in collaborative settings), the many hands problem is a recurring challenge in determining who is responsible for the outcomes of collective actions. In this context, it becomes essential to develop formal frameworks that can help apportion blame and responsibility among all involved parties, including both human and AI agents.

\subsubsection{Large Language Models as Judges}

% - Present LLMs as judges as a distinct use case where models evaluate, classify, or allocate blame among agents.
% - Highlight that this setting is normatively sensitive because the model’s judgments may influence accountability decisions.
% - Stress the importance of consistency, transparency, and alignment with human notions of blameworthiness.

Another important application of formal blameworthiness frameworks, we argue, is in the context AI systems that are used to evaluate, judge, or apportion blame among human agents. In such scenarios, LLMs may be used to assess the actions of individuals or groups and provide recommendations for accountability. However, this raises questions about the reliability and fairness of AI-generated judgments, as well as the ethical implications of delegating responsibility assessment to machines. By applying formal blameworthiness frameworks, we can better understand how LLMs can support human decision-making in these contexts while ensuring that their outputs align with established ethical principles. Some examples on these use cases are outlined in~\cite{dong_can_2024}, \cite{10.1093/jla/laae009}, and~\cite{posner2025judge}.

\subsubsection{Large Language Models Apportioning Blame}

% - Introduce the idea of LLMs not just judging blame but actively apportioning it among multiple agents.
% - Emphasize that this is a more complex task than binary blame assignment, as it requires reasoning about degrees of contribution and fairness.
% - Note that this task is particularly relevant in multiagent settings where outcomes are jointly produced and responsibility is shared.
% - Mention that LLMs are being increasingly used in decision support and advisory roles, which makes their ability to apportion blame a practical and ethical concern.
% - Highlight that this motivates empirical studies to evaluate how well LLMs can perform blame apportionment compared to human judgments and formal models.

LLMs excel at processing Natural Language and generating contextually relevant outputs, which makes them suitable for tasks that involve evaluating complex scenarios and reasoning about responsibility. In multi-agent settings, where outcomes are often the result of collective actions, LLMs can be used to apportion blame among agents based on their contributions to the outcome. This task requires the model to consider various factors, such as the agents' actions, the context of the decision-making process, and the potential consequences of those actions. 

Together with such applications come questions about the reliability and fairness of LLM-generated judgments, as well as the ethical implications of delegating responsibility assessment to machines. Although the question of wether LLMs can be considered morally responsible agents is particularly complex and controversial, since it involves philosophical, legal, and ethical considerations, we argue that it is possible to evaluate their behavior under simpler and more tractable settings. In particular, we can assess their ability to reason about blameworthiness in structured multi-agent scenarios, where the rules of the game are well-defined and the outcomes can be clearly measured.

Although we purposly avoid the question about moral judgement and alignment of LLMs on blame apportionment tasks, by leveraging formal blameworthiness frameworks, we can test to some extent whether LLMs can approximate or support such a framework which is designed to capture human notions of blameworthiness. We also remark that the FH framework is not the only formal framework for blameworthiness, but it is a particularly useful one for our purposes because it provides a clear and rigorous way to reason about blame in multi-agent settings.

In this work, we aim to compare the blameworthiness judgments made by LLMs with those predicted by the FH framework in a multi-agent committee scenario. By doing so, we can evaluate the extent to which LLMs can replicate or support human-like reasoning about responsibility and accountability in structured settings.

\subsection{Research Questions}\label{sec:research_questions}

With this background, the main question that this work aims to address is: to what extent do LLM-based judges reproduce the structure of FH blameworthiness in a multi-agent committee? We address this through two sub-questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1.} How does the blameworthiness assigned by the FH framework vary across its parameters (belief, vote, switch cost, stakes, pressure effectiveness, threshold, and group size), for the multi-agent committee scenario?
  \item \textbf{RQ2.} Do LLM-based judges placed in the same committee scenario assign blame that varies, in direction and in shape, as the framework's predictions specify?
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:committee_replication} illustrates the python implementation of the Committee of Voters example in a high-level diagram of its components. The implementation faithfully replicates the FH framework's calculations for blameworthiness in a multi-agent setting by following the original methodology. In this implementation, the epistemic state is represented as a probability distribution over possible causal settings, capturing the agents' beliefs about the likelihood of various outcomes based on their actions and the actions of others. The implementation allows for the specification of parameters such as belief, vote, switch cost, stakes, pressure effectiveness, threshold, and group size, enabling a comprehensive exploration of how these factors influence blameworthiness. 

The code is available in a public GitHub repository \cite{rcrtss2025miis} (particularly, in the \texttt{framework/committee.py} file), providing transparency and reproducibility for further research. Translating the abstract mathematics of the FH framework into functional code requires relying on the computational exhaustion of finite sets. For instance, to calculate the outcome probability (which represents the epistemic state) the script evaluates all $2^7 = 128$ possible voting combinations for the 7-agent committee. For each ``world'' where the outcome is achieved ($\ge 4$ \texttt{yes} votes), the algorithm computes its exact mathematical likelihood based on the base beliefs of the agents and calculates the cumulative probability. To compute group blameworthiness, the code programmatically searches the space of alternative actions for a given coalition. It calculates the coordination cost (e.g., applying social pressure or switching a vote) and the new outcome probability for every hypothetical action, evaluating the theoretical trade-off formula and storing the maximum resulting value across all valid alternatives.

Similarly, the combinatorial nature of the Shapley value equation is implemented through finite exhaustion to apportion individual blame. The algorithm isolates a target agent and generates every possible coalition of the remaining agents. For each subset, the script computes the group blameworthiness both with and without the target agent. The difference between these two values yields the agent's marginal contribution. Then, this contribution is multiplied by the Shapley weights (to account fairly for different coalition sizes) and summed across all permutations.

To address RQ1, we conduct a comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis of the FH framework. This analysis sweeps across the framework's parameters to observe their isolated and interacting effects on individual and group blameworthiness. This approach allows us to rigorously map the model's behavioral boundaries and sensitivity far beyond the single, static configuration presented in the original Committee of Voters example~\cite{friedenberg_blameworthiness_2019}, providing a more general understanding of how the framework behaves under a variety of conditions.

Specifically, we systematically vary seven key parameters: initial belief $p_0$ (the prior probability of an agent voting \texttt{yes}), the actual vote cast, personal switch cost $c_\text{switch}$, the stakes or balance parameter $N$, pressure effectiveness $\alpha$ (the marginal increase in probability per unit of social pressure), the cost of creating coalitions $c_\text{coalition}$, the voting threshold $T$, and overall group size $n$. The results of this analysis are presented in Section~\ref{sec:results}, where we discuss the observed patterns.

\subsection{LLM Judge Evaluation for Blameworthiness Apportionment}\label{sec:llm_judges}

The comparative evaluation of LLM-based judges for blameworthiness apportionment (addressing RQ2), while taking the results of the parameter sensitivity analysis into account, is the core objective of this work. As outlined in Section~\ref{sec:research_questions}, this evaluation aims to understand how well LLMs can approximate the blameworthiness assessments provided by the FH framework. 

The main idea behind this evaluation is to task a Large Language Model with the role of a judge who has to decide how blameworthy is a group of agents, and how to apportion that blame among the individual agents, given a specific scenario. Specifically, the LLM is provided with a description of the committee scenario, including a natural language representation of the parameters that define the agents' beliefs, votes, costs, and other relevant factors. The model is then asked to assign blameworthiness scores to each agent on a scale from 0 to 100, reflecting their perceived responsibility for the outcome.

Several challenges arise in such an evaluation. The following points summarize the most relevant obstacles and considerations considered in the design of the experiment:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Prompt Design:} The prompts used to elicit blameworthiness judgments from the LLM must be carefully crafted to ensure clarity and consistency. Variations in wording can lead to different interpretations of the scenario, which may affect the model's responses. Therefore, multiple prompt phrasings are tested to assess the robustness of the model's judgments. 
  
  \item \textbf{Scenario Diversity:} The evaluation includes multiple scenarios (see Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}) with different skins or framings. Each scenario presents unique challenges and contexts that may influence the model's blameworthiness assessments. Consistency across the results for the different scenarios is essential for drawing meaningful conclusions about the model's performance.
  
  \item \textbf{Natural Language Parameterization:} All parameter values must be expressed in natural language only, without using numerical representations in prompts. Ordinal labels are mapped to specific wordings, and the model's ability to interpret these mappings is itself a subject of the evaluation. This approach tests the model's capacity to reason about blameworthiness based on qualitative descriptions rather than quantitative inputs. (see Figure~\ref{fig:parameter_mapping} for an example on the mapping of ordinal labels to natural language descriptions).
  
  \item \textbf{FH Baseline Comparison:} Crucially, to recover the values from which the model reasoned, we also ask it for its own estimates of inferred parameters (e.g., $p_0$, $\alpha$, cost). This becomes highly relevant for the evaluation, as it provides a way of anchoring a given response to a specific parameter value, which is necessary for comparing the model's judgments to the FH framework's predictions.
  
  \item \textbf{Repetition Across Conditions:} The evaluation is repeated across different models, scenarios, phrasings, and repetitions to ensure that findings are robust and not artifacts of specific conditions or prompt designs.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

The experiment design was split in two parts: first, a headline sweep that manipulates the belief parameter ($p_0$) across all models and scenarios, and second, validation arms that involve one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) manipulations that change a single held variable (own vote, pressure effectiveness $\alpha$, and switch cost/stakes). The goal of the headline sweep is to test whether the LLM blame curve reproduces the shape predicted by the FH framework, while the validation arms check if the LLM curve shifts in the same direction as the FH baseline curve.

\paragraph{Headline Sweep} In total, the evaluation involved 3 models, 3 scenarios, 7 $p_0$ levels, 3 prompt phrasings, and 10 repetitions per condition, resulting in a total of 1,890 unique evaluations. Each evaluation consisted of presenting the model with a scenario description, including the relevant parameters expressed in natural language. The model was additionally asked to assign blameworthiness scores to each agent, an inferred value for the parameter $p_0$, and its inferred value for the increase in probability due to social pressure $\alpha$. The model's responses were then collected and analyzed to assess their alignment with the FH framework's predictions.

\paragraph{Validation Arms} The validation arms were run for one model (qwen) and one scenario (s01) only, resulting in a total of 210 unique evaluations. Each evaluation involved manipulating a single held variable (own vote, pressure effectiveness $\alpha$, and switch cost/stakes) while keeping all other parameters constant. The model was asked to assign blameworthiness scores to each agent, as well as its inferred values for the manipulated parameter. The model's responses were then collected and analyzed to assess whether the LLM curve shifted in the same direction as the FH predicted curve.

For the headline sweep, we assess whether the ranking of LLM blame scores across $p_0$ levels matches the FH prediction's ranking, and whether the blame curve shows the non-monotone peak predicted by the framework. For the validation arms, we test whether manipulating each held variable shifts the blame curve in the direction the FH baseline predicts. Where multiple comparisons are made, results are adjusted to control the false discovery rate.

The results of the headline sweep and validation arms are presented in Section~\ref{sec:results}, where we discuss the observed patterns and their implications for understanding how well LLM-based judges reproduce the structure of FH blameworthiness.

% The parameter sensitivity analysis reveals a set of structural properties of the FH FH baseline that are not immediately obvious from the framework's definition. The most salient is the \textbf{non-monotonicity of blame in belief} ($p_0$): individual blameworthiness peaks at moderate epistemic optimism ($p_0 \approx 0.35$, blame $\approx 0.128$) and declines sharply toward both extremes. When the agent is nearly certain others will vote yes, their marginal coalitional contribution is negligible; when nearly certain they will vote no, collective action is too costly to make a difference. Blame is highest precisely where individual pivotality and collective feasibility are jointly non-trivial.

% The remaining parameters modulate this curve in ways that are not always intuitive. \textbf{Own vote} shifts the curve downward but does not eliminate blame for the agent who voted correctly: the FH baseline assigns the yes-voter approximately 30\% of the no-voter's blame ($db_\text{yes} = 0.022$ vs. $db_\text{no} = 0.073$ at $p_0 = 0.6$), reflecting the lobbying action they could still have taken. \textbf{Switch cost} reduces blame monotonically, with the slope steeper at low stakes and flatter at high stakes, since the balance parameter $N$ discounts cost relative to the outcome. \textbf{Pressure effectiveness} ($\alpha$) shifts the location of the $p_0$ blame peak: lower $\alpha$ moves the peak rightward (blame peaks later, when belief is higher and lobbying finally becomes worth it), while higher $\alpha$ moves it leftward. At a fixed $p_0$, higher $\alpha$ actually decreases individual blame, as responsibility spreads across the coalition's Shapley contributions rather than concentrating on the focal agent. \textbf{Voting threshold} increases blame almost monotonically up to near-unanimity (6/7), where it peaks, then falls slightly at unanimity, as requiring all seven votes makes the alternative coalition too costly. Finally, under \textbf{group size} variation, individual blame dilutes as the group grows (0.244 for $n=3$ down to 0.044 for $n=9$), reflecting the ``many hands'' effect; group blame does not remain constant across group sizes.

% These patterns establish the FH baseline's ground truth for RQ2. The non-monotone $p_0$ curve and the partial blame of the yes-voter are the most counterintuitive predictions, and therefore the most diagnostic for assessing whether LLM judges reproduce the structure of the framework or default to simpler, monotone heuristics.

The parameter sensitivity analysis confirms expected structural properties of the FH framework, while revealing quantitative nuances (see Figure~\ref{fig:rq1_sensitivity} for an overview of the analysis results): 

First, the analysis shows that blame is non-monotonic in initial belief ($p_0$): it peaks at moderate optimism ($p_0 \approx 0.35$, blame $\approx 0.128$) and declines toward both extremes. This pattern follows directly from the framework's logic: blame depends on both pivotality and feasibility. When others are certain to vote \texttt{no}, intervention becomes too costly to achieve; when certain to vote \texttt{yes}, intervention is unnecessary. The peak occurs at intermediate beliefs where the individual agent can plausibly make a difference.

Second, the other parameters affect this base curve in predictable ways. For instance, an agent's \textit{actual vote} reduces the blame curve but does not eliminate it. The framework assigns the \texttt{yes}-voter roughly 30\% of the \texttt{no}-voter's blame (e.g., $db_\text{yes} = 0.022$ versus $db_\text{no} = 0.073$ at $p_0 = 0.6$). This reflects shared responsibility: even if an agent voted correctly, they could have still tried to pressure others to change their votes. \textit{Switch cost} decreases blame in a monotonic way, but the effect is smaller at high stakes ($N$); when outcomes matter more, the cost of switching matters less. 

Furthermore, \textit{pressure effectiveness} ($\alpha$) dynamically shifts the location of the $p_0$ peak. Lower effectiveness delays the peak rightward, meaning blame only materializes when baseline beliefs are already high enough to make weak lobbying viable. Counterintuitively, at a fixed belief state, higher pressure effectiveness actually \textit{decreases} the individual blame of the focal agent. Because the Shapley value calculates marginal contributions to coalitions, highly effective social pressure means that \textit{any} subset of agents could have fixed the outcome, thereby spreading the total responsibility more evenly across the group rather than concentrating it on the target agent. 

Finally, macroscopic variables like the \textit{voting threshold} and \textit{group size} behave in structurally sound ways. Blame steadily increases alongside the voting threshold, peaking at near-unanimity (6 out of 7 votes required), before dropping slightly at strict unanimity (7 out of 7), where the cost of coordinating a flawless consensus becomes too exorbitant to reasonably expect. Similarly, systematically increasing the group size (from $n=3$ to $n=9$) strictly dilutes individual blame (dropping from $0.244$ to $0.044$), successfully formalizing the philosophical ``problem of many hands.''

Ultimately, these patterns establish a rigorous, mathematically grounded baseline. The non-monotone belief curve and the partial blameworthiness of the ``correct'' voter represent predictions from the framework that may not be immediately intuitive, but that are philosophically sound. Because these findings oppose simple heuristics,  as assigning 100\% of the blame to the \texttt{no}-voter and 0\% to the \texttt{yes}-voter, they serve as an appropriate diagnostic benchmark for RQ2. They allow us to precisely evaluate whether Large Language Model judges can genuinely replicate the structural nuances of formal moral apportionment, or if they rely on shallower, monotonic heuristics that fail to capture the subtleties of shared responsibility in multi-agent settings.

\subsection{LLM-based Judges (RQ2)}

Before discussing the headline sweep and validation arms, it is important to comment about the input and output of the LLMs. As described in Section~\ref{sec:llm_judges}, the LLMs were provided with natural language descriptions of the parameters, and they were asked to assign blameworthiness scores to each agent on a scale from 0 to 100. The models were also asked to provide their inferred values for the parameters, which allows us to assess how well they understood the scenario and whether their blameworthiness judgments were consistent with their own inferred beliefs. The aggregated results accross models and scenarios showed that the mean inferred values for $p_0$ rise monotonically (from $\approx 0.13$ to $\approx 0.90$) as the swept level increases.

The results for the headline sweep of $p_0$ (RQ2) are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:rq2_llm_judges}. This figure shows the main finding of the study: accross all nine model-scenario combinations, the LLMs show a non-monotone pattern of blameworthiness that qualitatively matches the FH framework's predictions. Despite the fact that the models recieved natural language ordinal descriptions for $p_0$ rather than numeric values, they were able to capture the overall shape of the blame curve, peaking at moderate beliefs and declining toward both extremes. Some of the model-scenario combinations show clearly a single-peak pattern, while others show a more plateau-like behavior, but the overall trend is consistent with the FH framework's predictions in the sense that blame is highest at intermediate beliefs and lower at extreme beliefs. 

Furthermore, we calculated the Spearman rank correlation coefficient ($\rho$) between the LLM blame scores and the FH baseline for each model-scenario combination. The results show that eight out of nine cells statistically resemble the results from the FH baseline, except for the \texttt{gemini-2.5-flash} model in the board scenario ($\rho=0.10$). \texttt{Qwen3-235b-a22b-2507} shows the strongest agreement with the FH baseline for the health scenario ($\rho=0.53$), while \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-instruct} shows the weakest agreement for the grant scenario ($\rho=0.30$).

While the results show qualitative agreement in the direction of the FH blameworthiness computations, the quantitative difference between the LLMs and the FH baseline is substantial. Firstly, the LLM blame curves are compressed in magnitude compared to the FH baseline. The peak-to-trough range of the FH baseline is roughly $65.7\times$ (accross the seven levels of $p_0$), while the LLMs show a range of approximately $1.2-1.7\times$ (depending on the model-scenario pair). In other words, the LLMs significantly underestimate the variation in blameworthiness compared to the framework. 

Secondly, when comparing the blame scores against the FH value computed at its own inferred $p_0$ (oracle\_at\_inferred), the LLMs show weak agreement with the baseline. This suggests that the LLMs' judgements are not fully explained by the parameter values they report reasoning from, indicating that their blameworthiness assessments may be influenced by other factors or heuristics that are not captured by the FH framework's parameters.

The health scenario (s01) shows the strongest alignment between the LLMs and the FH baseline accross all models. On the other hand, the corporate board scenario (s03) shows the weakest alignment. Although the design of this experiment does not allow us to draw strong conclusions or causal claims about the effect of scenario framing on the LLMs' blameworthiness judgments, we do observe a big difference accross the scenarios, while preserving the non-monotone pattern of blameworthiness. This suggests that the framing of the scenario may influence the LLMs' judgments, and that further research is needed to explore this effect in more detail.

\subsection{Validation Arms}

For the validation runs, we manipulated three parameters: own vote, pressure effectiveness ($\alpha$), and switch cost/stakes. The results are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:rq2_validation_arms}. For simplicity and due to resource constraints, these validation arms were run for one model (\texttt{qwen3-235b-a22b-2507}) and one scenario (s01) only, so every direction-of-shift result rests on one cell and is indicative rather than confirmatory. 

For this scenario, when Dr. Aris votes \texttt{yes}, the LLM blame effectively shifts downward, as predicted by the FH baseline. Once more, the qualitative shift is preserved, but the magnitude of the shift is compressed compared to the FH baseline. Namely, for the FH baseline, the ratio of blame for an agent that votes yes, compared to the same agent voting no, is roughly $\approx 3.3\times$. For the LLM, the same ratio is $\approx 1.37\times$. Nevertheless, agents who vote yes are never assigned a score of zero, indicating that the LLM recognizes that they still have some responsibility for the outcome, just as the FH framework predicts. Revisiting the intuition on this point, LLMs seem to agree that even a correct vote does not absolve an agent of responsibility, as they could have still attempted to influence others to change their votes.

Similarly, when we manipuate the pressure effectiveness ($\alpha$), we observe that the LLM blame curve shifts in the same direction as the FH baseline. When $\alpha$ is increased, the blameworthiness peaks shift to the left. However, if we look back to Figure~\ref{fig:rq1_sensitivity}f, we can see that the FH baseline predicts that at a fixed $p_0$, blameworthiness should decrease as $\alpha$ increases. The LLMs do not capture this effect, as they show a slight increase in blameworthiness at a fixed $p_0$ when $\alpha$ is increased. This suggests that while the LLMs are able to capture the overall direction of the blame curve shift, they do not fully capture the nuanced effects of pressure effectiveness on individual blameworthiness.

Finally, when we manipulate the switch cost/stakes, we observe that blame curves from the LLM outputs are quite similar between each other, and no detectable shifts in blame can be observed. This is in contrast to the FH baseline, which predicts that blameworthiness should decrease as switch cost increases. Despite these results, it is important to note that the representation of the balance parameter $N$ in natural language may not have been interpreted by the LLMs as the experimental design intended. We note that this is a limitation of the study, and that further research is needed to explore how LLMs interpret and reason about natural language representations of parameters in blameworthiness frameworks. For the sake of this study, we limit ourselves to conclude that the non-monotone pattern of blameworthiness is preserved across the different tested values for cost/stakes, even if other effects from the FH baseline were not possible to detect.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}

% - Provide a detailed interpretation of the results from both RQ1 and RQ2, synthesizing the findings from the parameter sensitivity analysis and the LLM-based judges.
% - Discuss the implications of the results for understanding blameworthiness in multi-agent settings, and how they inform the broader questions of responsibility and accountability in AI systems.
% - Highlight any theoretical contributions, practical implications, or insights gained from the study, and how they relate to existing literature on blameworthiness and responsibility.

The sensitivity analysis of the FH baseline (RQ1) revealed that, under this framework, and for scenarios with the structure of the committee example, blameworthiness is non-monotonic in initial belief ($p_0$), peaking at moderate optimism and declining toward both extremes. Other parameters modulate this curve in ways that are not always intuitive, such as the effect of pressure effectiveness ($\alpha$) on the location of the peak, and the dilution of individual blame as group size increases. These findings establish a rigorous baseline for evaluating LLM-based judges (RQ2).

While using such a framework as baseline provides a principled way to evaluate LLMs, it is important to note the big challenges that come with this approach, particularly for framing the problem with natural language. 

The results answer RQ2 in a non-trivial way. The LLMs were able to capture the qualitative characteristics predicted by the FH framework, but notably the results differed quantitatively. This suggests that while LLMs can reproduce the results that align with intuitions on how humans apportion blame, there was no evidence about correct calibration, one that would be required for trusting LLMs with the magnitude of blameworthiness judgements.

In a sense, the two approaches partially validate each other: the FH framework provides a rigorous, mathematically grounded baseline that captures non-trivial patterns of blameworthiness, while the LLMs show qualitative agreement with this baseline, indicating that they can capture some structural nuances of moral apportionment (behaviors that likely emerge from the training data). However, the quantitative discrepancies suggest that LLMs may not be fully capturing the underlying principles of the FH framework, or that they may be influenced by other factors or heuristics that are not accounted for in the framework. This highlights the need for further research to explore how LLMs reason about blameworthiness and how their judgments can be calibrated and tested (e.g., through systematic evaluation against diverse scenarios and parameter settings, or even using other frameworks as baselines).

Despite this resemblance, the convergance between the LLMs and the FH baseline seems to be shallow. The LLMs' sensitivity to switch cost and pressure effectiveness did not match the FH baseline substantially. This can mean, we argue, two possible things: either the manipulation of these parameters in the design presents limitations that must be revised in future work, or the LLMs are not sensitive to cost and pressure in the same way that the FH framework suggests.


\subsection{How Closely LLM Blame Matches the Framework}

% "Right pattern, wrong magnitude"
% - This is the headline finding and deserves its own subsection so it is not diluted into general interpretation.
% - State the qualitative result: the models reproduce the *signature* of counterfactual (Shapley) blame -- non-monotonic in belief, peaking at moderate belief and declining toward certainty, sensitive to vote and to lobbying effectiveness -- and this is not an artifact of misreading inputs (inferred p0 rises monotonically with the swept level).
% - State the quantitative gap: the FH baseline's peak-to-trough range is compressed by the models (roughly 60x -> ~1.3-1.8x); a correct (yes) vote is never fully exonerated to zero; and blame is insensitive to switch cost.
% - Note that feeding each model its own inferred values (oracle_at_inferred) gives weaker agreement than the nominal mapping, so blame is not a clean function of the quantities the model reports reasoning from.
% - Note the framing effect: the cold corporate-board skin (s03) tracks the FH baseline most weakly, the affect-laden health skin (s01) most strongly.
% - Draw the implication for the LLM-as-judge use case: models capture the direction of structured blame but are not calibrated enough to be trusted with its magnitude.
The headline finding of this study is that LLM-base judges output the right pattern but the wrong magnitude when it comes to blameworthiness judgments in the context of the FH framework. Every model-scenario combination reproduces the signature of counterfactual Shapley blame, without being told to do so. Nevertheless, the models behave as if $p_0$ levels are in general similar in blame, while the framework assigns significantly more blame at moderate beliefs than at extreme beliefs.

The models also get several qualitative features right: a correct (yes) vote reduces but does not eliminate blame, and the blame curve shifts in the direction predicted by the FH baseline when pressure effectiveness is manipulated. However, the models fail to capture other effects, such as the decrease in blame with switch cost. Moreover, when we feed each model its own inferred values for $p_0$ and $\alpha$, we find that agreement with the FH baseline is in general week, suggesting that the models' blameworthiness judgments are not fully explained by the parameter values they report reasoning from. This indicates that the models may be influenced by other factors or heuristics that are not captured by the FH framework's parameters.

Finally, we observe that models are in general reliable for ordinal claims: which configurations attract more blame, whether the blame curve is non-monotone, whether changing a key parameter shifts blame in the expected direction. However, they are not reliable for cardinal claims: how much more blame a given configuration attracts, how much the curve shifts, or what the model's own inferred parameters are. 

Any application that requires precise quantitative apportionment, such as automated accauntability in AI governance, should not trust the magnitude of LLM blame scores without some external calibration.


\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limitations}

The most significant limitations of this study include the following:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Validation Arms for Single Model:} The validation arms were only run for one model (qwen) and one scenario (s01), which limits the generalizability of the findings. Future work should expand the validation arms to include multiple models and scenarios to confirm whether the observed patterns hold across different conditions. 
  
  \item \textbf{Parameter Manipulation:} The manipulation of parameters such as switch cost and pressure effectiveness may not have been interpreted by the LLMs as intended, which could affect the validity of the results. Future work should explore alternative ways to represent these parameters in natural language to ensure that they are understood correctly by the models.
  
  \item \textbf{FH Framework as Baseline:} While the FH framework provides a rigorous baseline for evaluating LLM-based judges, it is just one of many possible frameworks for moral apportionment. The findings may not generalize to other frameworks or scenarios that do not share the same structure as the committee example, and they certainly don't yield any claim about the moral correctness of the models' judgments. Future research could explore how LLMs perform when evaluated against different moral frameworks or in more complex, real-world scenarios.
  
  \item \textbf{FH Framework's Assumptions:} The FH framework relies on specific assumptions about how blameworthiness is calculated based on counterfactual contributions and the structure of the decision-making process. If these assumptions do not hold in real-world scenarios, the applicability of the findings may be limited. Future work could investigate how robust the results are to variations in the underlying assumptions of the FH framework.
  
  \item \textbf{FH Framework's Applicability to Real-World Scenarios:} The scenarios used in this study are stylized and may not capture the full complexity of real-world situations where blameworthiness judgments are made. Additionally, the simplification done by FH for the committee scenario to be computable may not reflect the nuances of real-world decision-making. Future research could explore how well the FH framework and LLM-based judges perform in more realistic settings, such as actual legal cases or ethical dilemmas faced by organizations.
  
  \item \textbf{Selection of LLMs:} The study only evaluated three LLMs due to resource constraints, which may not be representative of the broader range of models available. Future work should include a larger and more diverse set of models to assess whether the observed patterns hold across different architectures and training regimes.

  \item \textbf{Blame Score Range:} The LLMs were asked to assign blameworthiness scores on a scale from 0 to 100 due to the requirements of the experiment, which may not capture the full range of moral judgments that humans might make. Future research could explore alternative ways to elicit blameworthiness judgments from LLMs, such as using qualitative descriptions or more nuanced rating scales.

  \item \textbf{Balance Parameter $N$:} The balance parameter $N$ was reverse engineered but is not confirmed to be the one used by FH. Additionally, the natural language representation of this parameter is particularly complex due to the ambiguity of the concept itself, which may have led to misinterpretation by the LLMs. Future work could explore alternative ways to represent this parameter, or even consider using a different parameter that captures the same underlying concept but is more easily understood by the models.

  \item \textbf{Natural Language Representation of Parameters:} The parameters of the FH framework were represented in natural language for the LLMs, which may have led to misinterpretation or oversimplification. Future work could explore alternative ways to represent these parameters, such as using more structured prompts or providing additional context to ensure that the models understand the parameters as intended.

  \item \textbf{Scenario Framing Effects:} The study observed differences in LLM blameworthiness judgments across different scenarios, suggesting that the framing of the scenario may influence the models' judgments. Future research could systematically investigate how different framings affect LLM blameworthiness judgments and whether certain framings lead to more accurate or reliable assessments.
\end{itemize}

In general, future work should extend the validation arms to cover all models and scenarios, to confirm or refute the findings from the headline sweep. Testing more models accross more scenarios would provide a more comprehensive understanding of how LLMs reason about blameworthiness and how their judgments align with formal frameworks like FH. Additionally, exploring alternative ways to represent parameters and scenarios in natural language could help improve the accuracy and reliability of LLM-based judges. Finally, future research could investigate how LLMs perform when evaluated against different moral frameworks or in more complex, real-world scenarios, to assess the generalizability of the findings and their applicability to practical decision-making contexts.

\section{Conclusions}

This study provides a systematic evaluation of LLM-based judges in the context of blameworthiness apportionment, using the FH framework as a rigorous baseline. The findings reveal that while LLMs can capture the qualitative patterns predicted by the framework, they fall short in terms of quantitative accuracy and sensitivity to certain parameters. These results highlight both the potential and limitations of using LLMs for moral reasoning tasks, and underscore the need for further research to improve their calibration and reliability.

The practical implications of this work are in the context of AI governance and accountability, where LLMs may be used to assist in decision-making processes that involve moral judgments. The study suggests that while LLMs can provide valuable insights into blameworthiness, their outputs should be interpreted with caution, particularly when precise quantitative assessments are required.
